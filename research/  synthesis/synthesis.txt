# Pain Point Synthesis — Smart Onboarding

Compiled from 6 interviews: P01 (UX/AI Designer), P02 (Software Designer), P03 (Project Leader), P05 (Business Analyst), P06 (IT Support Analyst), P07 (Marketing Coordinator).

Contribution: Bishal Basnet, cross-checking pain points for technical feasibility ahead of hypothesis-writing.

---

## Theme 1 — Information is scattered across too many systems

Every participant described splitting their search across 3+ tools (Confluence, Teams, SharePoint, ServiceNow, email, wiki) with no single source of truth.

- P01: "Engineering primarily uses Confluence, designers use Figma, product managers frequently use Google Drive... There is no one source of truth."
- P03: "Documents were spread across SharePoint, Microsoft Teams, emails, and our internal wiki."
- P06: "Information is spread across several systems... sometimes useful information only exists inside old Teams conversations or even emails."

## Theme 2 — Version confusion / outdated documents

Almost every participant described finding multiple versions of the same document with no way to tell which was current.

- P01: guideline document existed in both Confluence and Teams, out of sync
- P03: found three different versions of a project scope document before a client meeting
- P05: "spent close to an hour" finding the right project template
- P06: outdated MFA troubleshooting guide in ServiceNow vs. correct one hidden in a manager-only SharePoint folder

## Theme 3 — Overwhelming first day, no prioritisation

Everyone described receiving access to many tools/systems on day one with no explanation of what mattered most.

- P02: "there wasn't much explanation about how everything connected together"
- P05: "a lot of information available, but very little guidance about what was actually important"
- P06: "a really long onboarding checklist. Everything seemed equally important"

## Theme 4 — Onboarding feels generic / box-ticking

Mandatory compliance training was consistently described as necessary but not useful for actual day-to-day work.

- P02: "mostly focused on completing mandatory tasks... didn't really prepare me for my everyday work"
- P06: "leaned more towards box-ticking"
- P07: "a lot of the mandatory training was quite general"

## Theme 5 — Not knowing who to ask / who owns what

- P02: "not knowing who to ask when I needed help" (top 2 problem)
- P01: "who had ultimate decision-making power and who owns various AI features"
- P06: had to ask "three or four different teams" to understand an approval workflow

## Theme 6 — No visible progress or clear next step

- P03 & P06: both said there were moments they'd finished their checklist but didn't know their next priority
- P05: "didn't always know what you were expected to do each day"

## Theme 7 — Manager check-ins were the most valued support

Where they existed, weekly manager check-ins were repeatedly named as the single most useful part of onboarding.

- P02: "weekly check-ins with my manager were probably the most valuable part"
- P07: "manager arranged weekly catch-up meetings... gave me confidence to ask questions"

## Theme 8 — AI trust requires transparency, and has clear limits

Every participant who used AI wanted source citations, last-updated dates, and confidence/reasoning shown. All independently named HR, salary, disciplinary, and legal matters as off-limits for AI.

- P01: "cite its sources, tell the reason for recommending something, and indicate how confident it is"
- P05, P06, P07: consistent requirement for links to official docs + last-updated timestamps
- Universal: HR/salary/disciplinary/legal → human only
